## Markdown, Zettlr, and Summarising

In the first class, I learnt about wiki and how it works. Although I have been using Wiki for almost my entire life, I wasn’t aware of its power and the social connection it provided. Wiki, the way I view it, is a huge notebook that the whole world can write on. Wiki connects the world by sharing information and correcting misinformation. Although the topic of Wiki itself is not new, the complexity of it was. 

I then proceeded to practice with Markdown and did an online exercise. Doing these exercises made me feel as if I was dealing with a complex version of word. It was really fun to see little symbols change how words, and sometimes, even entire phrases look different. Although it was simple at first, things were a little complicated towards the end with ordered and unordered lists in trying to remember the notations for them. Taking short notes on the Markdown notations really helped in not just understanding the concept, but also in referencing them when we worked with it in the following class.

After that, I downloaded Zettlr to further help me with my Markdown practice. With the combination of my skills and a bit of research, I figured out how to represent mathematical equations using LATEX format. It was really incredible to see complex mathematical functions be represented on a laptop. Although it was nothing like anything I’ve learnt before, it was pretty easy to get a hang of. The most challenging part was figuring out how to run the command on Zettlr. Every other programming platform I’ve ever worked with had an explicit button to run the code, but Zettlr did not. I spent a long time trying to look up how to run the code. Finally, I decided to stop wasting time on that and figure out how to write the code. When typing it out, I pressed the “Enter” button, and found out that that’s all I needed to do to get my code working. For me, reading through not just the snippets of commands that were required for the given mathematical equations, but also for a few others, helped give me context for how the LATEX format works.

Finally, me and my group split up the chapter 8 and 9 from the book The Information: A History, a Theory, a Flood by James Gleick and took notes summarising it. I mainly helped summarize the bit about Maxwell’s Sorting Demon. I found it really interesting how people came up with this concept of a little demon on every surface that requires its hot particles and cold particles to be separated.
